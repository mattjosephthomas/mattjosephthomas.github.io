Case in point — Algonquin Park

Over 800,000 people visit Algonquin every year — Canada's most iconic provincial park. The outfitters, lodges, and experience providers are world-class. The problem was never the product. It was the missing system connecting them to the people who wanted to come. I built that system — designing the itineraries, the access routes, and the overnight trip packages that now bring travellers from Toronto and beyond directly into Algonquin's economy.

Trusted by Parks Canada, Via Rail, and DMOs across Canada.

Point of View

Most destinations spend 90% of their budget on the 10% of the trip that happens after you park your car. Almost nobody thinks seriously about the conditions, infrastructure, and design decisions that determine who gets to show up at all. I close the gap between remarkable places and the people who never get to see them.

Looking ahead —

In five years, the winning destinations won't be the ones with the best ads. They'll be the ones with the fewest friction points between a traveller's desire and their arrival. I'm designing for that world now.

Signature Framework

The Four Types of People to Build a Destination

Every destination that works has four distinct types of people behind it. Most communities have some of them. Few have all four — and even fewer have them working together. This framework has guided destination design work across Canada.

1
The Pioneer

Pioneers go first. They create something new and prove there's demand — before anyone else believes it's possible.

2
The Local Leader

Local Leaders legitimize. Their endorsement turns a fringe experiment into a community project — and unlocks doors no outsider can open.

3
The Innovator

Innovators commercialize. They take the proven concept and build a scalable product — turning a good idea into a real economy.

4
The Master Planner

Master Planners coordinate. They connect the dots and ensure the growth serves the community — not just the visitor count.
